GLOBALISATION, ENTREPRENEURSHIP AND THE MODERN ECONOMY

The global economy has undergone(o'tmoq, boshidan kechirmoq) dramatic changes over the past century. From the industrial revolution(sanoat inqilobi) that transformed manufacturing to the digital revolution reshaping services and trade, economic systems have continuously evolved in response to technological change, political developments, and shifting consumer preferences(iste'molchilar afzalliklari). Today, we live in a world defined by increasing interconnectedness(o'zaro bog'liqlik), rapid innovation, and unprecedented(mislsiz) economic complexity.

Globalisation(globallashuv) — the process through which economies, cultures, and societies become increasingly linked — has been one of the defining forces of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. It has enabled countries to specialise(ixtisoslashmoq) in what they produce most efficiently, access larger markets, and benefit from the exchange of ideas and technologies. However, globalisation has also been controversial(bahsli, tortishuv mavzusi), as its benefits have not been equally distributed, and it has contributed to the displacement(siqib chiqarish) of workers in industries that cannot compete with cheaper international labour(ishchi kuchi).

1. The Rise of Entrepreneurship

1.1 What is Entrepreneurship?

Entrepreneurship(tadbirkorlik) — the process of creating a new business by taking on financial risk in hopes of profit — has become a central engine of economic growth and innovation. Entrepreneurs(tadbirkorlar) identify market gaps(bozordagi bo'shliqlar), develop new products or services, and create jobs. In recent decades, the rise of the internet and digital platforms has dramatically lowered the barriers to entry(kirish to'siqlari) for new businesses, enabling individuals to reach global markets at minimal cost. This has given rise to a new generation of start-ups(yangi kompaniyalar) that have disrupted traditional industries.

1.2 Social Entrepreneurship

A growing movement within entrepreneurship is social entrepreneurship(ijtimoiy tadbirkorlik) — the creation of businesses whose primary goal is to generate positive social or environmental impact rather than purely financial profit. Social entrepreneurs tackle issues such as poverty, access to clean water, education inequality, and climate change through innovative(innovatsion) business models. While these ventures often face unique challenges in securing funding(moliyalashtirish) and scaling their impact, they represent a growing recognition that business can be a powerful force for good.

2. Supply Chains and Global Trade

2.1 Modern Supply Chains

Modern products are rarely made in a single location. A smartphone, for example, may contain components(qismlar) sourced from dozens of countries, assembled in another, and sold globally. These complex supply chains(ta'minot zanjirlari) have enabled companies to minimise costs and access specialised(ixtisoslashtirilgan) expertise, but they have also created vulnerabilities(zaifliklar). The COVID-19 pandemic exposed these vulnerabilities dramatically: disruptions to global supply chains led to shortages(tanqislik) of everything from semiconductors to medical equipment.

2.2 Trade Agreements and Protectionism

International trade agreements(savdo shartnomalari) have played a crucial role in facilitating globalisation by reducing tariffs(bojlar) and other barriers to trade between countries. However, in recent years there has been a resurgence(qayta ko'tarilish) of protectionist(himoyalovchi) sentiment in many countries, driven by concerns about job losses, national security, and the desire to maintain strategic industries. This tension between free trade and protectionism remains one of the central debates in global economic policy.

3. Inequality and Economic Justice

3.1 Wealth Inequality

Despite the remarkable economic growth of recent decades, wealth inequality(boylik tengsizligi) has increased in many countries. The wealthiest 1% of the global population now owns more than half of the world's total wealth(boylik), while billions of people live in poverty(qashshoqlik). Economists debate the causes of this inequality — ranging from technological change and globalisation to tax policies and inheritance(meros) — as well as the most effective ways to address it. Proposed solutions include progressive taxation, strengthened social safety nets, and improved access to education and healthcare.

3.2 Sustainable Economic Development

Increasingly, economists and policymakers are recognising that sustainable development(barqaror rivojlanish) — growth that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs — must be at the heart of economic planning. This requires moving away from economic models that prioritise(ustuvor qilmoq) short-term growth at the expense of environmental health and social equity. The concept of a "circular economy(aylanma iqtisodiyot)," in which waste is minimised and resources are continually reused, is gaining traction as one component of a more sustainable approach.

Conclusion

The modern economy is characterised by extraordinary dynamism and complexity. Globalisation, technological innovation, and entrepreneurship have created remarkable opportunities for growth and prosperity. Yet they have also generated significant challenges — including inequality, environmental degradation, and economic insecurity(iqtisodiy beqarorlik). Addressing these challenges requires not just technical solutions but a fundamental rethinking(qayta ko'rib chiqish) of what we mean by economic success and who benefits from it.

A) The invention of the internet alone
B) Globalisation and increasing economic interconnectedness
C) The growth of protectionism
D) The decline of manufacturing industries

2. What has lowered barriers to entry for new businesses in recent decades?

A) Government subsidies for entrepreneurs
B) Reduced competition in global markets
C) The rise of the internet and digital platforms
D) Lower corporate tax rates worldwide

3. What did the COVID-19 pandemic reveal about global supply chains?

A) They were too expensive to maintain
B) They were resistant to all external shocks
C) They contained significant vulnerabilities to disruption
D) They worked perfectly during the crisis

4. What is the 'circular economy' concept?

A) An economy where all countries trade equally
B) A model where waste is minimised and resources are continually reused
C) A financial system based on cryptocurrency
D) An economy focused purely on services rather than manufacturing

5. What does the author suggest is needed to address modern economic challenges?

A) Simply creating more technology companies
B) Returning to closed national economies
C) Technical solutions combined with rethinking what economic success means
D) Eliminating all forms of international trade
