📝 Part 1 — Shaxsiy savollar

O'zingiz haqingizda gapirib bering

1
Do you have many friends or just a few close ones?
👁 Ko'rish
I prefer quality over quantity when it comes to friendship. I have a small but very close social circle — perhaps four or five people I would consider genuine friends. These are people I can trust completely and rely on in difficult times. I believe deep loyalty and mutual support define real friendship far more than the number of connections.
💡 Maslahat: Use 'quality over quantity' — examiners love this phrase
2
How do you usually make new friends?
👁 Ko'rish
I tend to form friendships naturally through shared experiences — studying together, working on projects, or participating in activities I enjoy. I'm not someone who makes friends instantly; I prefer to build trust gradually. Once there's a strong bond, however, those friendships tend to last a very long time.
💡 Maslahat: Describe your personality honestly
3
Is it easy to maintain friendships as you get older?
👁 Ko'rish
It definitely becomes harder. As people grow up, they move to different cities, start careers and families, and naturally have less free time. Long-distance friendships require more deliberate effort — regular messages, video calls, and occasional visits. Social media helps, but it can't fully replace face-to-face interaction.
💡 Maslahat: Acknowledge the challenge + offer solutions
4
What qualities do you value most in a friend?
👁 Ko'rish
Trust and loyalty are the qualities I value above everything else. A true friend is someone who supports you unconditionally, is honest even when honesty is uncomfortable, and shows genuine empathy when you're struggling. Mutual respect and the ability to resolve conflict without damaging the relationship are also essential.
💡 Maslahat: List qualities + briefly explain each one
5
Do you think social media has helped or harmed friendships?
👁 Ko'rish
Social media has made it easier to stay connected across distances, which is genuinely valuable for long-distance friendships. However, it has also created a culture of superficial connection — people collect followers rather than nurturing real bonds. I think it's a useful tool but shouldn't replace meaningful face-to-face companionship.
💡 Maslahat: Give balanced view: helped + harmed with examples
🎤 Part 2 — Uzoq monolog

1-2 daqiqa gapiring

Describe a good friend who is important to you

  • Who this person is
  • How you met
  • What kind of person they are
  • Why this friendship is important to you
1
Namuna javobni ko'ring
👁 Ko'rish
I would like to talk about my closest friend, whom I have known since we were both around ten years old. We met at primary school and have been inseparable ever since, which makes ours a true childhood friendship. What makes this person so special is their remarkable combination of honesty and kindness — they always tell me the truth, even when it is not what I want to hear, but they do so with genuine empathy and care. Our friendship has survived many challenges: disagreements, periods of living in different cities, and the inevitable changes that come with growing up. Through all of it, the trust and loyalty between us has never wavered. This friendship is important to me because it provides a sense of stability and belonging that is hard to find elsewhere. There is something incredibly reassuring about having someone who has known you for years, who accepts you completely, and who you know will always be there regardless of circumstance.
💬 Part 3 — Muhokama

Chuqurroq fikrlash va baholash

1
How have friendships changed in modern society?
👁 Ko'rish
Modern friendships are increasingly mediated by social media and digital communication. While this allows people to maintain larger social circles, many researchers argue that the depth of friendship has declined. The concept of companionship — spending genuine, uninterrupted time together — is less common as people are constantly distracted by screens and notifications.
💡 Maslahat: Reference research or trends to add depth
2
Do you think friendships between men and women are different from same-gender friendships?
👁 Ko'rish
I think friendships vary enormously between individuals, so generalisations are dangerous. That said, research does suggest some patterns — men's friendships often centre around shared activities, while women's friendships tend to involve more emotional disclosure. Cross-gender friendships can be particularly rich because they offer different perspectives and forms of support.
💡 Maslahat: Avoid stereotypes — acknowledge individual variation
3
Is it possible to have genuine friendship online?
👁 Ko'rish
I believe online friendships can be entirely genuine. Shared interests, mutual support, and meaningful conversation can absolutely develop through digital platforms. However, physical presence — the ability to share experiences in real life — adds a depth that online interaction alone struggles to replicate. The best online friendships tend to eventually become in-person ones.
💡 Maslahat: Give a nuanced yes with conditions
